So, Noah Caldwell-Gervais just put out a new video where he reviews From Software’s biggest three non-Dark Souls titles – Bloodborne, Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice, and Elden Ring. After I got over the excitement of getting another four whole hours of Noah’s admirable insight and enviable way with words, I got to a point in the video where he had begun discussing his perspective on the way FromSoft tends to approach difficulty, in that their games often allow players to subtlely tailor the game’s challenge to their skill level and preferences. This got me thinking about ways in which fighting games – a genre much maligned casually for its supposed high barrier of entry – could implement a similar system of tailoring mechanical complexity to suit a player’s needs. By which I mean, I remembered that Capcom Vs SNK 2 exists, and now I’m going to spend the next few minutes talking about it.

Something that I think has become a silent hallmark of Capcom’s approach to fighting game design is a level of mechanical customisability. Most recently, that’s just been picking your super, ultra, or V-System abilities, though it got its start in the much more far-reaching Ism System of Street Fighter Alpha 3, where players could choose from one of three fighting styles that changed the way they could interact with the game systems and characters. But I think the most well-realised version of that kind of mechanical customisability comes in the form of Capcom Vs SNK 2’s Groove system.

Capcom Vs SNK 2 had two primary intentions as a fighting game – expand on (and in some cases, address issues with) the first Capcom Vs SNK game, and celebrate the shared history of the two companies involved in the game’s creation. This leads us directly to the Groove System, which very intelligently combines equal amounts of game design callbacks and provision of specific options to allow players the ability to engage with the game’s systems in different ways depending on playstyle, mechanical familiarity, perceived strength, and indeed, ease of use. Thus, the six Grooves – C, A and P-Groove for the Capcom side, and S, N and K-Groove for the SNK side.
